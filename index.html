Research

Current Research

I develop physics-informed thermal models for the ePIC Silicon Vertex Tracker (SVT) at the future Electron-Ion Collider. The SVT uses corrugated carbon fiber disks to support silicon sensors and facilitate passive air cooling. My work focuses on quantifying heat dissipation and conduction across the carbon structure using heater mockups that simulate realistic LEC (high-power) and RSU (low-power) components.

I collect spatially-resolved temperature measurements under different heater configurations and build nearest-neighbor thermal network models that account for:

  • environmental heat loss,
  • in-plane lateral conduction,
  • vertical cross-layer coupling between sensor rows.

My model incorporates geometric offsets, power density differences, and spatial dependence of convective loss. It successfully predicts the temperature profile on one sensor layer based on the activity of neighboring rows, even in unmeasured configurations — providing a tool to guide future design and cooling optimization of the SVT.

Past Research

At the sPHENIX experiment at RHIC, I improved monitoring displays for the data acquisition system to help shift crews ensure the quality of incoming data. This included visualizations for packet size (to track detector readout contribution) and bunch crossing number (to verify timing system alignment with the beam).

I also worked with the CERN ATLAS Open Data project, developing and documenting Jupyter notebooks that introduce students to machine learning techniques for analyzing proton-proton collisions. These tools are now used in Masters-level lab courses.
